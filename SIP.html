Hypothesis

Review of related literature

Compost is a mixture of organic matter such as banana peels, orange skins, etc. To soil, compost is an all organic fertilizer which is cheap, as well as effective. It even helps with balancing Ph level, enriching the soil, suppressing pests, and discouraging certain weeds. To the environment, compost helps by reducing greenhouse gasses and creating healthy soil. Finally, for plants, compost helps roots expand, water drain, and air enter clay or silt soil by loosening firmly linked particles.

Compost also encourages healthy root systems to be cultivated with compost, which reduces runoff. Lastly, since compost includes helpful microorganisms that protect plants from diseases and pests, it can help to decrease the need of chemical pesticides. In short, compost is an environmentally friendly, cheap, organic fertilizer with benefits and is most of the time even better than artificial fertilizers.

The plant our group will focus on is Raphanus Sativus. Nutrients needed by radishes are Potassium which strengthens plants, contributes to early growth and helps retain water. It also affects the plant’s disease and insect suppression, Potassium is metabolized by plants through a process known as root interception, in which roots extend through the soil profile to make contact with nutrients. Phosphorus is responsible for assisting with the growth of roots and flowers. Phosphorus also helps plants withstand environmental stress and harsh winters. Through diffusion, which is the transfer of a nutrient ion from a location of high concentration to an area of lower concentration, phosphorus gets to the root surface.

Raphanus Sativus gives nutrients that are needed for our body to be healthy and the elements that were needed for its optimal growth are Potassium to be strengthened. Raphanus Sativus also has iron which can resist disease.

Fruit peels are a good source of nutrients for Raphanus Sativus. Radishes have plenty of phosphorus so if farmers intend to add fertilizer before sowing,they can use something like Banana, and Mango peels. These are some examples of fruit peels that include phosphorus and other nutrients needed to help Raphanus Sativus’ growth.

Mango skins are rich in nutrients like Vitamin A, C, K, folate, magnesium, choline, potassium. Banana peels are 3.25 percent phosphorus, one of the other major nutrients that plants need to grow. Phosphorus helps rooting, improves winter hardiness and speeds up flowering and fruiting.

Chop your fruit peels, scraps and trim pieces smaller before composting them, especially tough varieties such as citrus peels and melon rinds. They'll decompose quickly, with less risk of creating odors or attracting pests.

(Soaking the banana peels allows the nutrients to leech into the water, and once it hits the soil, the roots grab all the minerals.)

Bury your fruit peels at least 10 inches deep in the pile. Covering the fruit waste will also help reduce odor and pest problems. Layer your fruit scraps with carbon-rich "brown" ingredients such as straw, hay, dry leaves or sawdust.

These balance the nitrogen-rich fruit, helping keep the pile balanced and its beneficial bacteria active.
